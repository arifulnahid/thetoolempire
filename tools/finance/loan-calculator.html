How to Use This Calculator

From filling in your loan details to reading the full amortization table.

1
Pick a Loan Type
Click a tab — Mortgage, Auto, Personal, Student, or Business — to pre-fill typical interest rates and term lengths for that loan type. Switch to Custom for any values.
2
Enter Loan Amount
Type the principal (the amount you're borrowing, before interest) or drag the slider. Choose your currency from the dropdown — 12 currencies supported.
3
Set Rate and Term
Enter the annual interest rate and loan term in years and months. The sliders let you adjust quickly; the numbers update instantly as you drag.
4
Try Extra Payments
Add an extra monthly payment amount to see how many months you save and how much interest you avoid. This is one of the most powerful ways to reduce total loan cost.
5
Compare Terms
The Compare section shows what your payment and total interest would be for other common term lengths — so you can weigh monthly payment size against total cost.
6
Read the Amortization Table
Toggle the amortization schedule on to see every month's payment split into principal and interest, plus the remaining balance. Load 12 rows at a time or show all at once.

Frequently Asked Questions

The monthly payment uses the standard PMT (payment) formula from finance: P × [r(1+r)ⁿ] / [(1+r)ⁿ − 1] where P is the loan amount, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n is the total number of payments. This produces a fixed payment that fully pays off the principal and interest by the last month. If the interest rate is 0%, the payment is simply principal ÷ n.

This is how amortization works. Interest is calculated on your remaining balance each month. In the early months your balance is high, so the interest charge is high — leaving less of your fixed payment to reduce the principal. As the balance drops over time, the interest portion decreases and the principal portion grows. This is called a "front-loaded" amortization structure. The amortization table in this calculator shows exactly how this plays out month by month.

Extra payments reduce your principal faster, which reduces the amount of interest charged in every subsequent month. The effect compounds. On a 30-year mortgage at 6.5%, adding just $200/month in extra payments can cut roughly 5–7 years off the term and save over $60,000 in interest depending on the balance. The earlier in the loan term you start making extra payments, the more powerful the effect — because the balance is highest at the start.

The interest rate is the annual cost of borrowing the principal, expressed as a percentage. APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is a broader measure that includes the interest rate plus fees, points, mortgage insurance, and other charges — rolled into a single annual percentage. APR is always equal to or higher than the stated interest rate. This calculator uses the stated interest rate. If you have an APR figure, use it as the rate for a closer estimate of your true cost of borrowing.

No — this calculator assumes a fixed interest rate for the entire loan term, which is accurate for fixed-rate mortgages, auto loans, personal loans, and most student loans. For an ARM, you can use this tool to calculate the payment for the initial fixed period, then recalculate separately for each rate adjustment period with the new rate and remaining balance.

This calculator computes principal and interest only. For a mortgage, your actual monthly outlay is typically higher because it also includes property taxes, homeowners insurance, and possibly PMI (private mortgage insurance if your down payment is under 20%) and HOA fees — often bundled into an escrow payment. For auto loans, add the cost of insurance. For personal and student loans, check whether origination fees apply. The calculated total interest is the pure cost of the loan, excluding these additional costs.

The comparison table shows what your monthly payment and total interest would be for 10, 15, 20, 25, and 30-year terms at the same rate and principal. Shorter terms have higher monthly payments but significantly less total interest. Longer terms have lower monthly payments but you pay much more in interest over time. This helps you find the balance between affordable monthly payments and minimizing total cost.

An amortization schedule is a complete table of every loan payment, showing: the payment number and date, the total payment amount, how much goes to interest that month, how much reduces the principal, and the remaining balance. It lets you see exactly where you are in the loan at any point in time and how the principal/interest split changes over the life of the loan. Toggle the schedule on using the switch in the results panel.
