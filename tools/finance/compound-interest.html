Frequently Asked Questions

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal each period: Interest = P × r × t. Compound interest is calculated on both the principal and the accumulated interest from all previous periods. This means your balance earns interest on interest — creating exponential, not linear, growth. Over long periods, the difference is dramatic. At 7% annual rate, $10,000 becomes $19,672 with simple interest over 30 years, but $76,123 with monthly compounding.

Compounding frequency is how often interest is calculated and added to your balance. Annual compounding adds interest once a year; monthly adds it 12 times; daily adds it 365 times. Each time interest is added, it becomes part of the balance that earns interest in the next period. More frequent compounding means interest-on-interest kicks in sooner and more often. The difference between monthly and daily compounding is small; the big jump is from annual to monthly.

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the stated annual interest rate without accounting for compounding. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) is the effective annual return after compounding is applied. For example, a 7% APR compounded monthly has an APY of approximately 7.229% because each month's interest earns interest in subsequent months. APY always equals or exceeds APR. When comparing savings accounts or investments, APY is the more accurate number to compare because it reflects what you actually earn in a year.

The Rule of 72 is a quick mental math shortcut to estimate how long it takes an investment to double. Divide 72 by your annual interest rate to get the approximate doubling time in years. At 6%, money doubles in roughly 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years. At 9%, it doubles in about 8 years. It's a rough estimate but surprisingly accurate for rates between 2% and 15%. The exact calculation uses the formula: t = ln(2) / ln(1 + r).

If contributions happen at the start of each period (annuity due), each contribution earns interest for that entire period before the next one starts — resulting in slightly higher returns. End-of-period contributions (ordinary annuity) is the more common default, used by most automatic savings and 401(k) payroll deductions. The difference is one extra compounding period per contribution. For a $500/month contribution at 7% over 30 years, start-of-month yields roughly $6,000 more than end-of-month.

Inflation erodes the purchasing power of money over time. Even at 3% annual inflation, something that costs $100 today costs about $243 in 30 years. To "break even" against inflation, your investment must grow faster than the inflation rate. The inflation-adjusted (real) value shows what your future balance would be worth in today's dollars: Real Value = Nominal Value / (1 + inflation)^years. This is often called the "real return" — what you actually gain in purchasing power, not just nominal dollars.

Common benchmarks: high-yield savings accounts (4–5% in 2024–2025), S&P 500 average historical return (~10% nominal, ~7% real after inflation), diversified stock portfolio (~7–8% real), bonds (~3–5%), and money market funds (~4–5%). For retirement planning, many financial advisors use 6–7% as a conservative real return for a balanced stock/bond portfolio. The presets in this calculator use rates commonly cited for each scenario — but your actual returns will vary.

In tax-advantaged accounts like a 401(k), Traditional IRA, or Roth IRA, growth is either tax-deferred or tax-free, allowing the full compound effect shown here. To approximate after-tax returns in a taxable account, reduce your annual rate by roughly your marginal tax rate × expected annual return (e.g., if you're taxed 25% on a 7% return, use ~5.25%).

About This Tool

Compound Interest Calculator computes final balance, total interest earned, and year-by-year growth for any combination of principal, annual rate, compounding frequency, and time period. Supports regular contributions.

🛠

How to Use

  1. Enter the principal (initial deposit).
  2. Set the annual interest rate (%).
  3. Choose compounding frequency: daily, monthly, quarterly, or annually.
  4. Enter the number of years and any regular contribution amount.
  5. View the total balance, interest earned, and a year-by-year growth table.
⚙️

Logic & Algorithm

Uses the compound interest formula: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) where P = principal, r = annual rate, n = compounds per year, t = years. With regular contributions C added each period: A = P(1+r/n)^(nt) + C × ((1+r/n)^(nt) − 1) / (r/n). Each year row is calculated iteratively to show step-by-step growth.
